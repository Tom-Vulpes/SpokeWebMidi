🔌 Connect your SPOKE board

The SPOKE board has 27 touch pins in a circle. When you touch a pin — or connect something conductive to it — it tells the computer to play a sound.

What you need

🖥️ A computer
🔌 USB cable
🟢 SPOKE board
🌐 This webpage

How to connect

  1. Plug the USB cable into your SPOKE board
  2. Plug the other end into your computer
  3. Your computer sees it as a MIDI device
  4. Press "Connect MIDI" below
  5. Pick your SPOKE board from the list
Test it by touching a pin — you'll see it light up on the spoke!
Computer → USB → SPOKE board

✂️ Make your touch sensors

Now you know what sounds you have, you can design sensors to match! Each pin senses when something conductive touches it. Connect your craft sensors with crocodile clips.

Materials

🐊 Crocodile clips
🪙 Tin foil
✏️ Soft pencil (6B)
🍌 Fruit / veg
📎 Paper clips
🎨 Card & paper
🧲 Conductive tape
🌿 Leaves / flowers

Connecting a sensor

  1. Clip one end of a crocodile clip onto a pin on the SPOKE board
  2. Clip the other end onto your sensor (foil, fruit, pencil drawing…)
  3. Press the reset button on the board to calibrate
When you press reset, the board shows a rainbow animation while it calibrates the sensor levels. Don't touch any sensors while the rainbow is going! Wait until it stops, then your sensors are ready.
Anything that conducts a little electricity will work — metal, fruit, wet things, graphite pencil marks, even your friends' hands!
Pin → croc clip → sensor material. Then press reset!

Sensor ideas for your story

  1. Foil lightning bolt → thunder sound
  2. Pencil footprints on card → footstep sounds
  3. A real leaf → wind or forest sounds
  4. Cup of water (dip wire in) → splash sounds
  5. Tin foil sword → sword clash
  6. A friend's hand! → hold hands to trigger a sound
⚡ The signals are tiny and completely safe — like static from a balloon!

📖 Tell your story!

Your sensors are connected, your sounds are loaded — perform!

How to perform

  1. Tell your story out loud
  2. When something happens, touch the right sensor to play its sound
  3. Have a friend be the sound operator while you narrate!
  4. Practice a few times — it gets better!
Write your story out and mark where each sound goes. Write the pin number next to each sound cue!
